01 · Problem

The Research Question

Additive manufacturing (AM) has the potential to fundamentally change how products are designed, produced, and distributed — but significant barriers prevent this potential from being realised at scale. Access to AM equipment, knowledge, and quality-assured processes remains fragmented and unequally distributed across the manufacturing ecosystem.

The research question explored how a cloud-based platform architecture could aggregate distributed AM capacity — connecting designers, engineers, and manufacturers through a digital infrastructure that reduces access barriers, improves utilisation of existing AM assets, and enables new models of distributed production.

02 · Research Approach

Methodology

The dissertation adopted a design science research methodology — combining systematic literature review with stakeholder analysis and strategic framework development. Key activities included:

  • Comprehensive literature review of AM platform models, Industry 4.0 frameworks, and manufacturing-as-a-service business models
  • Stakeholder ecosystem mapping — identifying actors across the AM value chain and their roles, incentives, and interdependencies
  • Platform architecture design — defining layers of the proposed system from physical AM assets through digital services to end-user interfaces
  • Strategic analysis using established frameworks (value chain analysis, platform theory, SWOT) applied to the proposed model

03 · Platform Architecture

The Proposed Framework

The proposed platform architecture comprises four interconnected layers:

  • Physical layer: Distributed AM assets (FDM, SLA, SLS machines) across participating manufacturing sites, standardised through common quality and capability registries
  • Digital integration layer: API-driven connectivity enabling file transfer, machine status monitoring, and production tracking across geographically distributed nodes
  • Service layer: Design validation, material selection guidance, and quality verification services operating as cloud functions accessible to all platform users
  • User interface layer: Engineer and designer-facing tools for job submission, quoting, tracking, and quality reporting

The architecture prioritises modularity — allowing new AM nodes and service providers to join the ecosystem without disrupting existing participants — and standardisation of data formats and quality metrics across the distributed network.

04 · Key Insights

Research Findings

Governance is the primary challenge, not technology

The technical infrastructure for a distributed AM platform is largely achievable with existing cloud technologies. The harder problem is governance: quality standards, liability, intellectual property, and trust between participants in an open manufacturing network.

SME participation requires active barrier removal

Small and medium manufacturers — who hold much of the distributed AM capacity — face disproportionate barriers to platform participation. Successful platform design must actively lower onboarding costs and reduce compliance burden for SME nodes.

Data standardisation enables network effects

The value of the platform grows non-linearly with the number of participants, but only if data formats, capability descriptors, and quality metrics are standardised across nodes. Establishing these standards early is a prerequisite for network effects.

05 · Lessons Learned

What I Took Away

01

Engineering management bridges technical and strategic thinking. This project required deep understanding of both AM technology and platform business models — demonstrating that the best engineering solutions require management thinking, not just engineering thinking.

02

Systems thinking scales. The tools used to model a car chassis — understanding load paths, interfaces, and interdependencies — apply equally to modelling a manufacturing ecosystem. Systems thinking is a transferable engineering skill.

03

The future of manufacturing is distributed. Industry 4.0 is not just about smart factories — it is about smart networks of factories. Engineers who understand both the technology and the system-level implications will be well placed to shape this transition.
