Amora Collective · Product Design Internship · 2025

01 · Context

The Brief

Amora Collective is a product company with an existing range of products. The internship brief centres on redesigning existing products to improve their manufacturability, reduce production complexity, and align design intent more closely with manufacturing reality.

This is applied product development work — not academic. Every design decision has a direct implication for how the product is made, what it costs to produce, and how consistently it can be manufactured at scale.

02 · Role & Responsibilities

What I Am Working On

01

Product redesign

Analysing existing product designs and developing redesigned versions that maintain or improve function while simplifying the manufacturing process and reducing production complexity.

02

DFM analysis

Applying Design for Manufacture principles to identify features that are difficult or costly to produce, and proposing design changes that achieve the same functional outcome through more manufacturable geometry or process choices.

03

Manufacturing feasibility

Reviewing designs against manufacturing process capabilities — assessing tolerances, material choices, and assembly sequences to ensure designs are producible within the company's manufacturing constraints.

04

Design iteration and review

Working iteratively with the team — proposing changes, receiving feedback, and refining designs through structured review cycles typical of a professional product development environment.

03 · Engineering Approach

How I Am Approaching It

The starting point for any redesign is understanding why the current design is the way it is — what constraints shaped it, what problems it solves, and where the manufacturing friction comes from. Jumping to a solution without this understanding risks creating new problems while solving old ones.

DFM is not just about simplifying geometry. It requires understanding the full manufacturing context: which processes are available, what tolerances are achievable, how the product is assembled, and where variation in production will cause quality issues. Good DFM reduces cost and improves consistency without compromising the product's intended function or user experience.

04 · Engineering Judgement

Key Decisions So Far

Understanding before redesigning

Before proposing any design changes, time was invested in understanding the existing product's manufacturing process end to end — identifying where production friction actually occurs rather than assuming where problems might be.

DFM as a constraint, not an afterthought

Manufacturability considerations are being built into the redesign from the first concept sketch — not reviewed at the end. This avoids the common pattern of designing a product and then discovering it cannot be made as intended.

Preserving design intent while improving production

The redesign brief requires improving manufacturability without losing what makes the product work or look right. This balance — between production efficiency and design integrity — is the central tension the work navigates.

06 · Early Lessons

What I Am Learning

Professional product design is iterative by nature. Every design goes through multiple review cycles before it is approved. The discipline of structured iteration — propose, review, refine — is what separates professional product development from a one-shot design exercise.

DFM requires manufacturing knowledge, not just design knowledge. Understanding what is difficult to make requires understanding manufacturing processes deeply — not just in theory, but in terms of real process capabilities, tolerances, and failure modes.

Communication is a design skill. Presenting design rationale clearly — explaining why a decision was made, not just what was decided — is as important as the design itself in a professional product development environment.
