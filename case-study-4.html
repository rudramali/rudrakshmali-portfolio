BE Mechanical Engineering · Final Year Project

01 · Problem

The Challenge

Conventional loading bays are constrained to fixed orientations — limiting their flexibility in logistics environments where goods must be handled from multiple directions. The project brief required the design and engineering of a multi-directional rotating loading bay capable of receiving and dispatching loads from different approach angles.

The challenge was both mechanical — designing a rotation mechanism that could support the required load while enabling smooth, controlled multi-axis movement — and structural: ensuring the platform could withstand operational loads without fatigue failure or excessive deflection.

02 · Constraints

Engineering Constraints

  • Load capacity: Platform required to support defined static and dynamic load cases representative of logistics handling
  • Deflection limits: Maximum allowable deflection under full load to maintain safe operation
  • Factor of safety: Minimum FoS defined for all structural members under worst-case loading
  • Manufacturability: Prototype to be built in university workshop using standard materials and processes
  • Compactness: Mechanism required to fit within defined envelope constraints

03 · Design Process

Concept to Prototype

01

Mechanism concept generation

Explored multiple mechanisms for achieving multi-directional rotation — including turntable bearings, crossed-roller arrangements, and segmented track systems. Each was evaluated against load capacity, compactness, and manufacturability criteria.

02

CAD development in SolidWorks

Selected concept developed into a full 3D assembly model with all components, fasteners, and interfaces defined. Assembly-level clearance checks confirmed the mechanism would function through its full range of motion without interference.

03

FEA structural validation

ANSYS Mechanical used to run static structural analyses across the defined load cases. Stress and deflection results mapped across all components, with high-stress regions identified for design review. All structural members confirmed above minimum FoS requirements.

04

Prototype manufacture

Workshop drawings generated from the CAD model and submitted for fabrication. Prototype manufactured using CNC machining, manual turning, and standard fasteners. Assembly completed and validated against the CAD model.

05

Testing and validation

Physical prototype tested under representative loading conditions. Measured deflection values compared against FEA predictions — confirming model accuracy and structural adequacy.

04 · Key Decisions

Engineering Judgement

Bearing selection

Selected a large-diameter slewing ring bearing as the primary rotation element — accepting higher unit cost compared to a fabricated track system, in exchange for significantly improved load capacity, compactness, and reliability. The commercial bearing also reduced manufacturing complexity substantially.

Platform material — steel vs aluminium

Chose mild steel structural sections for the platform frame rather than aluminium — the welding simplicity, cost, and stiffness-to-cost ratio of steel outweighing the weight saving from aluminium in this application, where weight is not a performance driver.

FEA mesh refinement strategy

Applied locally refined mesh at weld joint locations and areas of geometric stress concentration, rather than globally refining the mesh — achieving accurate stress results at critical locations without the computational cost of a fully fine mesh.

05 · Results

What Was Delivered

The project delivered three key outputs: a complete SolidWorks CAD assembly with full engineering drawings, an ANSYS FEA validation report confirming structural adequacy across all load cases, and a working physical prototype demonstrating multi-directional rotation under load.

Measured test deflections compared well against FEA predictions, validating both the modelling approach and the structural design. The prototype demonstrated smooth, controlled rotation in multiple directions — meeting the original design brief.

06 · Lessons Learned

What I Took Away

01

The gap between CAD and manufacture is real. Tolerances that look fine in SolidWorks can be challenging to achieve in a workshop. Building the prototype taught me to design with tighter awareness of practical manufacturing limits.

02

FEA is a tool, not a guarantee. The validation process — comparing FEA predictions against physical measurements — demonstrated both the power and the limits of simulation. Boundary conditions and simplifications matter enormously.

03

Complete the engineering story end-to-end. Taking a project from brief through design, analysis, manufacture, and physical test gave me a complete view of the product development cycle that no individual stage alone could provide.
